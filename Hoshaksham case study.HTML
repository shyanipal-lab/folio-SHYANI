The Product

What is Hoshaksham?

Ho Saksham (Hindi for "become capable") is a SaaS tool designed for independent service providers — yoga teachers, tutors, personal trainers, coaches — who manage subscribers and need to collect recurring payments without an accounting degree.

The core idea: make it feel like money is being stored in the app, not like filling out a government form. Built around trust, simplicity, and the reality that most small service providers run their businesses from WhatsApp.

Meet Priya

Our one user. Our whole north star.

Priya is a 30-something yoga teacher running her own studio. She has 15–20 regular students, monthly subscription plans, and an intense hatred of the phrase "just send the invoice." We built an empathy map to deeply understand her world before touching any wireframes.

What Priya Says
  • "I want to focus on my yoga classes, not paperwork."
  • "It's exhausting to track payments manually every month."
  • "I can't remember who has paid or who I need to follow up with."
  • "Why can't this process be simpler?"
What Priya Does
  • Uses spreadsheets to track subscriptions but struggles to keep them updated.
  • Manually follows up with students via WhatsApp to ask for payments.
  • Spends hours creating invoices and ensuring they're sent correctly.
  • Occasionally forgets reminders, resulting in late payments.
What Priya Thinks
  • "Am I missing any payments this month?"
  • "I hope I don't offend my students by constantly asking for money."
  • "This admin work is taking time away from what I love — teaching yoga."
  • "If I could automate this, life would be so much easier."
What Priya Feels
  • Frustrated: She dreads repetitive subscription management.
  • Overwhelmed: Disorganised tracking makes her feel out of control.
  • Anxious: Worrying about missing payments creates stress.
  • Hopeful: She believes a solution like Hoshaksham could change everything.
The Problems

Three things making Priya's life unnecessarily hard

💸
Asking for money — the awkward bit
Keeping track of who had paid and when was a constant anxiety. Priya had to manually check her spreadsheet, then message students individually. Uncomfortable and error-prone.
📊
Manual tracking in spreadsheets
Service providers relied on spreadsheets to manually track subscribers. This worked until it didn't — which was roughly the third time someone updated the wrong row.
📄
Invoice generation — manual, monthly, miserable
Priya had to manually generate invoices monthly or per-service. Time-consuming, inconsistent, and honestly beneath someone who is also running an actual business.
❓
Uncertainty about who has actually paid
No single source of truth. Priya didn't know the current payment status without digging through WhatsApp messages, spreadsheet rows, and her own memory.
Design Psychology

The principles that shaped the decisions

Rather than applying UX laws rigidly, we used them as lenses to stress-test our thinking. Context-specific insights always won over blanket rules.

Hick's Law
Reduce cognitive load
The dashboard shows only relevant, actionable information. Fewer choices = faster decisions. Priya shouldn't have to think about what to do next — the interface should guide her.
Zeigarnik Effect
Make incomplete tasks visible
Unpaid invoices and pending reminders surface prominently on the dashboard. The discomfort of seeing an unfinished task motivates action — a gentle, guilt-free nudge.
Existing Habit
Make it feel like money
The core design metaphor: money being stored in the app. Not forms. Not admin. People are familiar with the idea of money going somewhere — we leaned into that mental model.
The Solution

What we actually built

💳
Payment link via WhatsApp
Send a payment link directly to students through WhatsApp. Payment lands in Priya's bank account. No awkward "did you pay?" messages required.
🧾
Automated monthly invoices
Generate and send invoices automatically. Priya sets it up once. The system handles the rest every month. Hours of admin: gone.
👥
Subscriber & plan management
Create plans, add subscribers, track who's active and who's lapsed — all in one clean dashboard. No spreadsheet expertise required.
📊
Payment overview dashboard
At a glance: total received, pending, upcoming. Priya knows her money status in three seconds without opening a spreadsheet.
🔔
Smart notifications
Reminders for unpaid invoices surface on the dashboard (Zeigarnik Effect in action). Never forget a follow-up again — the app does it for you.
🏦
Direct bank account connection
Link a bank account once. All payments route directly. No middleman apps, no manual transfers. The money just… arrives.

Competitive Analysis

When there are no direct competitors

No product existed that solved Priya's exact problem — subscription management for small independent service providers with a WhatsApp-first payment flow. The closest competitor was Zoho Billing, which is built for businesses 10x Priya's scale and complexity.

This was both a validation that the problem was real and underserved, and a reminder that we had to design from first principles rather than benchmarking existing solutions. When the competition is a sledgehammer, build a scalpel.

The Impact

What changed for Priya

📉
Fewer errors
Automated tracking eliminated the manual entry errors that plagued the spreadsheet approach. Priya's payment data is now accurate without any extra effort.
⏱️
Time savings
Automated invoices and payment links removed hours of monthly admin. Time Priya can now spend teaching, not typing.
😌
Reduced stress
Knowing exactly who has paid and when — without needing to check — fundamentally changed Priya's relationship with the admin side of her business.
🔔
Zero missed reminders
The notification system means Priya never forgets to send a reminder or invoice. The Zeigarnik Effect at work: incomplete tasks don't disappear, they surface.

Lessons

What this built from scratch actually taught me

01
One deep interview beats a hundred shallow surveys
Working with a single user (Priya) meant we understood her completely. Every design decision traced back to something real she said or did. Qualitative depth over quantitative breadth — especially at zero stage.
02
Start small, iterate fast
Building around one user's feedback gave us focus and speed. We weren't designing for an abstract user type — we were solving for a specific person with a specific frustration. The clarity made every decision easier.
03
Apply psychology thoughtfully, not literally
Hick's Law initially pointed us toward maximum simplification. But deeper context revealed that Priya needed organisation, not just reduction. Understanding when to deviate from the rule is the real skill.
04
Constraints breed creativity
Working with Figma's free tier and experimenting with Framer forced creative problem-solving. Without design system shortcuts, we had to rely on manual consistency checks and reusable components. Turned out to be great discipline.
